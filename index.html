Why the answer is what it is

Different paths from $-2$ to $+2$ — the imaginary part depends on which side of the pole you go

Pick a path. The real part of the answer is always 4 (because $\int 1\,dz$ only depends on endpoints). But the imaginary part changes — it's $\pm \pi$ times 2 depending on whether the path winds under the pole, over it, or all the way around.

Why the imaginary part = 2π × (winding around z=0)

$\int 2/z\,dz = 2 \log z$ along the path, where $\log$ is multi-valued. Tracking $\log z$ continuously along a path, the imaginary part jumps by $\Delta(\arg z)$, which is $2\pi$ times the (signed) winding number around the origin. So:

$$\int_C \frac{2}{z}\,dz = 2 \cdot i \cdot \Delta(\arg z) = 4\pi i \cdot (\text{winding}).$$

For the closed loop (winding 1) you get $4\pi i$ — exactly $2\pi i \cdot \text{Res}_{z=0}\,f$, since the residue of $1+2/z$ at $0$ is 2. Cauchy's residue theorem in action.

The math, condensed

Solve it on paper

Step 1. The integrand splits cleanly: $\dfrac{z+2}{z} = 1 + \dfrac{2}{z}.$ Integrate the two pieces.

Step 2 — first piece. $\displaystyle\int_C 1\,dz$ is a perfect differential, so

$$\int_C 1\,dz = z(2\pi) - z(\pi) = 2 - (-2) = 4.$$

Step 3 — second piece. Plug in $z=2e^{i\theta}$, $dz = 2i e^{i\theta}\,d\theta$:

$$\int_C \frac{2}{z}\,dz = \int_\pi^{2\pi} \frac{2}{2e^{i\theta}} \cdot 2i\,e^{i\theta}\,d\theta = \int_\pi^{2\pi} 2i\,d\theta = 2\pi i.$$

Notice the $e^{i\theta}$ cancels — that's why this is a textbook case.

$$\int_C \frac{z+2}{z}\,dz \;=\; 4 + 2\pi i.$$

Power function on the upper semicircle

Problem 2. $f(z) = z^i = e^{i\,\mathrm{Log}\,z}$ — the principal branch of the power function, with $\arg z \in (-\pi, \pi)$. Let $C$: $z(\theta) = e^{i\theta}$, $\theta \in [0, \pi]$. Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_C f(z)\,dz$. Hint: explain why the integral is well-defined even though $f$ is not defined at $\theta = \pi$.

Answer: $\displaystyle\int_C z^i\,dz = \tfrac{1+e^{-\pi}}{2}(i-1) \approx -0.5216 + 0.5216\,i$

Concept 1 · the integrand

What does $z^i$ even mean? An interactive playground

By definition, $z^i = e^{i\,\mathrm{Log}\,z}$. Plug in $\mathrm{Log}\,z = \ln|z| + i\arg z$:

$$z^i = e^{i(\ln|z| + i\arg z)} = e^{-\arg z}\cdot e^{i\ln|z|}.$$

So $|z^i| = e^{-\arg z}$  (magnitude depends only on the angle of $z$) and $\arg(z^i) = \ln|z|$  (angle depends only on the magnitude of $z$). Drag the dot to feel it out.

Concept 2 · the branch cut

Why $f$ is undefined at $z=-1$, and why the integral is fine anyway

The principal branch picks $\arg z \in (-\pi, \pi)$ — so the negative real axis is the branch cut. Approaching it from above ($\arg \to \pi^-$) and from below ($\arg \to -\pi^+$) gives values $2\pi$ apart, so $\mathrm{Log}\,z$ is discontinuous there. The point $z=-1$ on $C$ corresponds to $\theta=\pi$ — exactly on the cut, so $f(-1)$ isn't defined by the principal branch.

But the integral is still well-defined, because $|f|$ stays bounded on the entire upper semicircle:

$$|f(e^{i\theta})| = e^{-\theta}, \qquad e^{-\pi} \le |f(e^{i\theta})| \le 1.$$

A bounded integrand with one missing value (a single point — measure zero!) is integrable. The limit $\lim_{\theta\to\pi^-} f(e^{i\theta}) = -e^{-\pi}$ exists, so we just integrate using the continuous extension. That's the resolution of the hint.

$|f(e^{i\theta})|$ on $C$  ·  bounded between $e^{-\pi}$ and $1$, finite limit at $\theta=\pi$

Concept 3 · $f$ along the contour

$f(z(\theta)) = e^{-\theta}$ — a real, shrinking number

Substitute $z = e^{i\theta}$ into $f$. On the unit circle $\ln|z|=0$, and for $\theta\in[0,\pi)$ the principal $\mathrm{Log}\,z$ is just $i\theta$. So:

$$f(z) = e^{i\,\mathrm{Log}\,z} = e^{i\cdot i\theta} = e^{-\theta}.$$

Sweep $\theta$ from $0$ to $\pi$. The point on $C$ moves up and around to $-1$, while $f(z)$ marches down the real axis from $1$ toward $e^{-\pi}$ — never leaving the real line.

The math, step by step

Tap each step to reveal

Step 1 — parameterize $C$

$z(\theta) = e^{i\theta}$, $\theta \in [0, \pi]$. Differentiate: $dz = i\,e^{i\theta}\,d\theta$.

Step 2 — compute $\mathrm{Log}\,z$ on $C$

$|z| = 1$ so $\ln|z| = 0$. For $\theta \in [0, \pi)$, the principal $\arg z = \theta$, well inside $(-\pi, \pi)$. Hence $\mathrm{Log}\,z = i\theta$.

Step 3 — simplify $f(z(\theta))$

$f(z) = e^{i\,\mathrm{Log}\,z} = e^{i \cdot i\theta} = e^{-\theta}$. Real and decreasing. The boundedness $|f| \le 1$ is what saves the integral at the cut.

Step 4 — assemble the integrand

$f(z)\,dz = e^{-\theta} \cdot i\,e^{i\theta}\,d\theta = i\,e^{(i-1)\theta}\,d\theta$.

Step 5 — integrate $\theta$ from $0$ to $\pi$

$\displaystyle\int_0^\pi i\,e^{(i-1)\theta}\,d\theta = \frac{i}{i-1}\,\bigl[e^{(i-1)\theta}\bigr]_0^\pi = \frac{i}{i-1}\,\bigl(e^{(i-1)\pi} - 1\bigr).$

Step 6 — simplify

$e^{(i-1)\pi} = e^{i\pi}\cdot e^{-\pi} = -e^{-\pi}$,  and  $\dfrac{i}{i-1} = \dfrac{1-i}{2}$ (rationalize the denominator).

$\displaystyle = \frac{1-i}{2}\,\bigl(-e^{-\pi} - 1\bigr) = \frac{(1+e^{-\pi})(i-1)}{2}.$

$$\boxed{\;\int_C z^i\,dz = \frac{1+e^{-\pi}}{2}\,(i - 1) \approx -0.5216 + 0.5216\,i\;}$$
Cross-check via antiderivative

Since $\frac{d}{dz}\!\bigl[\frac{z^{i+1}}{i+1}\bigr] = z^i$, write:

$\displaystyle\int_C z^i\,dz = \left.\frac{z^{i+1}}{i+1}\right|_{z=1}^{z=-1\,\text{(from above)}} = \frac{-e^{-\pi} - 1}{i+1} = \frac{(1+e^{-\pi})(i-1)}{2}. \checkmark$

Computed at $z=-1$ as a limit along the upper semicircle, where $\mathrm{Log}\,z\to i\pi$, so $z^{i+1} = e^{(i+1)\cdot i\pi} = e^{-\pi}\cdot e^{i\pi} = -e^{-\pi}$.

Concept check · 5 questions

Test your understanding

Click an answer. Green = correct, red = wrong, explanation appears.

1. For any $z \ne 0$, what is $|z^i|$?

$z^i = e^{i\,\mathrm{Log}\,z} = e^{-\arg z}\cdot e^{i\ln|z|}$, so $|z^i| = e^{-\arg z}$. It depends on the angle of $z$, not its magnitude.

2. On the unit circle ($|z|=1$), $z^i$ is...

$\arg(z^i) = \ln|z| = \ln 1 = 0$, so $z^i$ has zero imaginary part. It's purely real, with $z^i = e^{-\arg z}$ — the function $e^{-\theta}$ in our problem.

3. $f$ is undefined at $z=-1$ ($\theta=\pi$). Why is $\int_C f\,dz$ still well-defined?

A bounded function's integral is unaffected by behavior on a measure-zero set, and a single point has measure zero. Here $|f(e^{i\theta})| = e^{-\theta} \le 1$ on all of $[0,\pi]$, with finite limit $-e^{-\pi}$ at $\theta = \pi$ from above. (Note: $f$ does not extend continuously across the cut — values from above and below differ by $e^{2\pi}$.)

4. After substitution the integrand is $i\,e^{(i-1)\theta}\,d\theta$. The antiderivative is...

$\int e^{a\theta}\,d\theta = e^{a\theta}/a$. Here $a = i-1$ and there's a leading $i$, giving $\dfrac{i}{i-1}\,e^{(i-1)\theta}$.

5. The final value of $\int_C z^i\,dz$ is...

Plugging into Step 5: $e^{(i-1)\pi} = -e^{-\pi}$, $\dfrac{i}{i-1} = \dfrac{1-i}{2}$, product is $\dfrac{(1-i)}{2}(-1 - e^{-\pi}) = \dfrac{(1+e^{-\pi})(i-1)}{2}$. Numerically $\approx -0.5216 + 0.5216 i$.
